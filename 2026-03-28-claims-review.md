# Claims Risk Review — RaapTech Construction Agency Launch
**Reviewer:** TopG (CRO, RaapTech LLC)  
**Date:** 2026-03-28  
**Subject:** Customer-facing marketing claims — pressure test before launch

---

## Executive Summary

Of 8 claims reviewed: **3 GREEN**, **4 YELLOW**, **1 RED**. Nothing that kills the launch — but two claims need immediate rework before any paid ads or outbound copy go live. The guarantee claim is the live wire; do not use it as written.

---

## Claim-by-Claim Review

---

### 1. "Save 10+ hours per week on admin tasks"
**Risk Level: 🟡 YELLOW**

**Why it's risky:**  
Specific quantified promise without a documented baseline. If a client tracks their time and hits 6 hours, you've got a disappointed client — and potentially a chargeback argument. "10+" sounds authoritative but is unsubstantiated without case study data. The FTC's guides on endorsements and testimonials (16 CFR Part 255) require that typical results be disclosed if atypical ones are advertised.

**Recommended phrasing:**  
> "Clients typically reclaim **6–12 hours per week** on admin and coordination tasks — results vary by team size and current workflow maturity."

Or softer:  
> "Most clients identify **significant weekly time savings** within the first 30 days of implementation."

**Legal note:** Once you have 2–3 real client results, replace this with a sourced range ("Client X saved 11 hrs/wk after workflow audit"). That converts Yellow to Green immediately.

---

### 2. "4-6 consultation spots available per month" (scarcity framing)
**Risk Level: 🟡 YELLOW**

**Why it's risky:**  
Scarcity framing is legally fine *if it's true*. The FTC and most state AGs treat fake scarcity as deceptive advertising. If you fill 8 spots in a month or never turn anyone away, the claim becomes false advertising. Additionally, if you're running this through a landing page funnel, some ad platforms (Google, Meta) flag hard scarcity claims for manual review.

**Recommended phrasing:**  
> "We limit new engagements to **4-6 per month** to maintain the quality of every client engagement."

This reframes scarcity as *quality control*, not artificial urgency — and it's true: Tim can only do ~5 quality engagements/month at the engagement depth proposed.

**Legal note:** Keep a log. If you ever take on 7+ in a month, update the copy. Maintain an internal policy doc that defines capacity. That paper trail matters if anyone ever challenges it.

---

### 3. "$3K-$5K for a 5-10 day engagement" (pricing positioning)
**Risk Level: 🟢 GREEN**

**Why it's safe:**  
This is a price range with a corresponding scope range. Nothing deceptive here — ranges are standard B2B consulting pricing. The 5-10 day window aligns with the dollar range (lower end = shorter engagement). No FTC concerns. No implied outcome. Clean.

**Notes:**  
- Make sure your SOW/contract defines clearly what "day" means (billable hours vs. calendar day).  
- Add a line about what's *not* included (travel, 3rd-party software, extended implementation) to prevent scope creep disputes.
- Consider adding a discovery call disclaimer: "Final pricing confirmed after scoping call."

---

### 4. "Construction Workflow Audit + First Automation" (delivery promise)
**Risk Level: 🟡 YELLOW**

**Why it's risky:**  
"First Automation" is doing heavy lifting here without definition. If a client expects a fully deployed, tested automation and you deliver a documented process map with one Zapier zap, that's a gap. Ambiguity in deliverable language is the #1 cause of B2B service disputes.

**Recommended phrasing:**  
> "**Construction Workflow Audit** — full documentation of current admin workflows, bottleneck identification, and a prioritized improvement roadmap — **plus implementation of your first automation**, scoped during the engagement."

Or in proposal language:  
> "Deliverables include: (1) Workflow audit report, (2) Automation opportunity ranking, (3) One automation fully implemented and tested within the engagement window."

**Legal note:** Whatever you say publicly, your SOW must match it. Misalignment between marketing language and contract is how disputes start. Get a template SOW reviewed by a contract attorney ($300–500 one-time) before you hit client #5.

### 5. "20 years of Autodesk Fabrication experience" (credibility claim — TRUE)
**Risk Level: 🟢 GREEN**

**Why it's safe:**  
It's true. Verified. Specific. This is exactly the kind of credibility anchor that converts in B2B. Autodesk Fabrication is a niche enough product that anyone in construction tech will immediately understand the implied expertise level.

**Notes:**  
- Can be strengthened with: "20 years of hands-on Autodesk Fabrication experience across MEP and industrial construction workflows."  
- If Tim holds any Autodesk certifications (even legacy ones), add them — they function as third-party validation.
- Consider a one-liner bio format for landing pages: "Tim Raap brings 20 years of Autodesk Fabrication experience to every engagement — the same tools your team already uses."

---

### 6. "Currently serving 4 active construction clients" (TRUE)
**Risk Level: 🟢 GREEN**

**Why it's safe:**  
Social proof, and it's accurate. Totally standard and clean. Small client count actually reinforces the "boutique, high-touch" positioning — it's a feature, not a weakness, at this stage.

**Notes:**  
- Don't name clients without written consent (NDA risk, referral relationship risk).  
- If you can get even one client to provide a 1-sentence quote, that converts this from a stat to a testimonial — much higher conversion weight.
- Update this number as you grow. Stale social proof (e.g., still saying "4" when you have 10) is a minor trust erosion.

### 7. "ROI within 90 days" (if used)
**Risk Level: 🔴 RED**

**Why it's a red:**  
This is the most dangerous claim on the list. "ROI within 90 days" is a specific, measurable, time-bound financial promise. Problems:

1. **ROI is almost impossible to isolate.** Construction projects span months. Labor costs, project mix, crew changes, and market conditions all affect outcomes. Proving your automation caused the ROI — or that a client didn't achieve it — is a legal nightmare.
2. **90 days is too short for most construction workflows.** Crews need to adopt the new process, managers need to stop reverting to old habits, software needs to stabilize. Real ROI evidence often takes 60–180 days just to measure accurately.
3. **If a client misses 90 days, you own the conversation.** Even if you're not legally liable, you're now managing a disappointed client relationship.
4. **FTC risk**: Financial outcome claims require substantiation. "ROI within 90 days" with no asterisk and no typical-results disclosure is textbook FTC concern territory.

**Do not use this claim without:**  
- At least 3 documented client case studies showing ROI
- A clear definition of what "ROI" means (time savings x hourly rate? direct cost reduction?)  
- A disclosure like "based on client X achieving Y"

**Alternative if you need urgency/outcome language:**  
> "Most clients identify their first measurable efficiency gains within the first 30 days."

Or (once you have case data):  
> "Client X reduced admin overhead by 8 hours/week — at a $45/hr blended rate, that's a full engagement cost recovered in under 60 days."

---

### 8. "Guaranteed time savings or we'll extend support free" (if used as guarantee)
**Risk Level: 🟡 YELLOW** *(borderline Red — read carefully)*

**Why it's risky:**  
The guarantee itself is not illegal — it's actually a solid trust-building move in B2B services. The risk is in the details:

1. **"Time savings" is unmeasured at contract start.** Without a pre-engagement time baseline (documented), there's no way to objectively evaluate whether the guarantee triggered. You'll be arguing over feelings, not data.
2. **"Extend support free" has no cap.** Unlimited free support is an open-ended liability. One bad client or one genuinely difficult workflow could cost you weeks of unpaid time.
3. **Clients who invoke guarantees are already disappointed.** This is a relationship management problem before it's a legal one.

**How to make it Green:**  
If you want to use a guarantee, define every term:

> "If we don't identify at least **[X] hours/week** of automatable admin time in your workflow audit — documented against your pre-engagement baseline — we'll extend our support engagement by **up to 5 additional days** at no charge."

This version:
- Anchors the guarantee to the *audit finding* (something you control), not the *client's ultimate result* (something you don't)
- Caps the liability (5 days, not "unlimited support")
- Requires a baseline measurement (protects both parties)

**Legal note:** Any guarantee language should appear in your SOW/contract, not just marketing copy. If it's in an ad but not in the contract, you have a misalignment problem.
